# Leverage

## What is Leverage?

Leverage is a tool that allows traders to control a large position with a relatively small amount of capital. It's expressed as a ratio (e.g., 100:1) and amplifies both potential profits and losses.

## How Leverage Works

Leverage enables you to trade positions larger than your account balance by borrowing funds from your broker.

**Example:**
- Account balance: $1,000
- Leverage: 100:1
- You can control: $1,000 × 100 = **$100,000** worth of currency

## Leverage Ratios Explained

### Common Leverage Levels
- **1:1** - No leverage (full amount required)
- **10:1** - Conservative (10% margin required)
- **50:1** - Standard (2% margin required)
- **100:1** - Common retail level (1% margin required)
- **200:1** - High leverage (0.5% margin required)
- **500:1** - Very high leverage (0.2% margin required)

### Margin Requirement Calculation
```
Margin Required = Trade Value ÷ Leverage Ratio
```

**Example:**
- Trade value: $100,000
- Leverage: 100:1
- Margin required: $100,000 ÷ 100 = **$1,000**

## The Double-Edged Sword

### Advantages of Leverage
1. **Capital Efficiency**
   - Control large positions with small capital
   - Opportunity for higher returns

2. **Increased Trading Opportunities**
   - Access to markets with high entry costs
   - Ability to diversify with limited capital

### Risks of Leverage
1. **Amplified Losses**
   - Losses can exceed your initial deposit
   - Rapid account depletion possible

2. **Margin Calls**
   - Higher leverage increases margin call risk
   - Positions may be closed automatically

3. **Emotional Stress**
   - Large position sizes can increase emotional pressure
   - May lead to impulsive decisions

## Real-World Example

### Without Leverage
```
Account: $10,000
Position: $10,000 (1:1 leverage)
Price moves 1%: $100 profit/loss
```

### With 100:1 Leverage
```
Account: $10,000
Position: $1,000,000 (100:1 leverage)
Price moves 1%: $10,000 profit/loss (100% of account)
```

## Regulatory Considerations

### Regional Limits
Different countries have different leverage restrictions:
- **EU/UK**: 30:1 maximum for major currency pairs
- **US**: 50:1 maximum for major pairs
- **Australia**: Up to 500:1 (varies by broker)
- **Asia**: Often 100:1 or higher

### Risk Warnings
Brokers are required to display warnings about leverage risks, including:
- Percentage of retail clients that lose money
- Risk of losing all invested capital
- Importance of risk management

## Best Practices for Using Leverage

### 1. Start Small
- Begin with lower leverage (10:1 or 20:1)
- Gradually increase as you gain experience

### 2. Use Proper Position Sizing
- Never risk more than 1-2% of account per trade
- Calculate position size based on stop loss distance

### 3. Maintain Adequate Margin
- Keep sufficient free margin for market fluctuations
- Monitor margin levels regularly

### 4. Implement Stop Losses
- Always use stop loss orders
- Set stops based on technical levels, not margin requirements

### 5. Understand Your Risk Tolerance
- Assess how much you can afford to lose
- Choose leverage that matches your risk profile
